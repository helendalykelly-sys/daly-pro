### Verification Notes

**Note 1 — RB-16: Central Bank Act 1942, s.33AQ.**
Verified against the *Central Bank Act 1942 Revised* (Law Reform Commission consolidation, updated to 10 July 2023). Section 33AQ sits in **Part IIIC, Chapter 2** and carries the statutory heading **"What decisions Bank can make at conclusion of inquiry held under section 33AO."** It was originally inserted (1 August 2004) by the Central Bank and Financial Services Authority of Ireland Act 2004 (No. 21 of 2004), s.10, and was most recently substituted (19 April 2023) by the Central Bank (Individual Accountability Framework) Act 2023 (No. 5 of 2023), s.45 (S.I. No. 176 of 2023) — it remains a live provision.

On a finding (on the balance of probabilities) that a person is committing or has committed a prescribed contravention, s.33AQ(3) empowers the Bank to impose one or more sanctions: (a) a caution or reprimand; (b) a direction to refund or withhold money charged for a financial service; (c) a monetary penalty not exceeding the prescribed amount; (ca)–(cb) suspension or revocation of authorisation (non-ECB-authorised firms); (cc)–(cd) a proposal to the ECB to suspend or withdraw authorisation (ECB-authorised firms); (d) disqualification of a natural person from performing controlled functions; (da) conditions on a natural person's controlled functions; (e) a direction to cease the contravention; and (f) a costs direction. Under s.33AQ(4) the prescribed monetary-penalty cap is the greater of €10,000,000 or 10% of turnover for a body corporate or unincorporated body, and €1,000,000 for a natural person. Subsections (5)–(6) provide parallel sanctions for persons who participated in a contravention while performing a controlled function.

**Conclusion:** RB-16 verified. The table's description ("Central Bank power to impose administrative sanctions") is accurate; the precise statutory heading and Part/Chapter location are recorded above.

**Note 2 — RB-17: SIMI Code and dispute-resolution framework.**
Verified against SIMI consumer-help materials at simi.ie ("SIMI Standards" and "Complaints Service" pages). Findings:

- SIMI is a **non-statutory trade association**; members subscribe to a member **Code of Ethics** requiring high standards of ethical conduct and customer service.
- SIMI operates a **free Consumer Complaints Service** and an **Arbitration scheme** covering the purchase, service or repair of vehicles by retail member companies, excluding matters subject to a manufacturer's warranty claim.
- A complaint must be referred to SIMI **in writing, with supporting documentation, within 3 months** of the date the cause of complaint first manifested itself.
- Where mediation does not resolve the matter, it is referred to the **SIMI Retail Motor Industry Standards Tribunal**.
- No separately published, version-numbered "SIMI Code of Practice — Retail Motor Industry" document was located on simi.ie. The applicable instruments are the member Code of Ethics and the published Consumer Complaints Service / Arbitration procedures; the table's Instrument cell has been updated to reflect this.

**Caveat:** simi.ie pages could not be retrieved directly (server returned HTTP 403); content above was confirmed via indexed search summaries of the simi.ie/en/consumer-help pages. A direct check of simi.ie is recommended before formal reliance, and the current published procedures should be re-confirmed at the time of use.

**Note 3 — RB-12 to RB-15: Consumer Protection Code 2012 (currency check).**
Currency-checked 21 May 2026 against the Central Bank of Ireland. The Central Bank published the Consumer Protection Code 2025 on 24 March 2025; it came into effect on **24 March 2026** and replaces the Consumer Protection Code 2012 (as amended) in its entirety. As of this check the 2012 Code is therefore **no longer the current in-force code**.

The 2012 Code nonetheless remains the applicable instrument for conduct that occurred while it was in force — i.e. before 24 March 2026. For conduct on or after 24 March 2026 the Consumer Protection Code 2025 applies (given effect through the Central Bank's 2025 Consumer Protection Regulations and accompanying Code), and its structure differs from the 2012 Code's chapter scheme. Which Code governs a particular complaint therefore depends on when the relevant conduct took place: RB-12 to RB-15 remain valid citations for pre-24 March 2026 conduct, and a parallel set of CPC 2025 references should be added for any conduct from that date onward.
